Projects

Structured citations for the sum of all human knowledge

We are building a structured repository of citable sources that can serve Wikipedia and other open knowledge projects.

Project overview

Verifiability is one of the core elements that make open knowledge projects in general and Wikipedia in particular work—to be credible, information must be verifiable and backed by reliable sources. Even though citations play a central role in Wikipedia, the method used to cite information on the platform was outdated and buried citations in the body of an article. In turn, this made citations were very hard to maintain or discuss, poorly discoverable, and very difficult to reuse or analyze.

As a result, there has been growing interest in building a stronger technological foundation for representing sources that enables contributors to store and reuse citations across Wikimedia projects. While many attempts have been made to realize this vision, previous efforts failed to address the issue of representing citations and source metadata in a well-structured machine-readable format due to both immature technology and fragmented community involvement.

Only with the introduction of Wikidata—a central storage repository for structured data from Wikimedia projects for which citations play a vital role—did efforts around improving citations begin to coalesce. In 2016, the WikiCite project was started by Wikimedia Research as an initiative aiming to build an open repository of sources that can serve all open knowledge projects and Wikipedia at large leveraging Wikidata as its primary infrastructure.

The Wikicite team aims to develop a deeper understanding of how Wikimedia contributors use sources and lay the foundation for better technological support around sources and citations. In addition to improving citations across Wikimedia projects, a structured repository of sources will enable researchers to link information claims with underlying sources in Wikidata, begin studying the quality and nature of sources across articles and Wikipedia language editions, as well as identify biases and gaps in information sources.
